Innovation and Product Development: IP and OSD solutions compared

Intellectual Property, or IP, has been the industry standard solution for creating new and innovative technologies. The definition of intellectual property is: property (such as an idea, invention, or process) that derives from the work of the mind or intellect. Intellectual property rights are important as they allow an individual to lay claim to their intellectual property which can then be used to profit off their ideas. For centuries, intellectual property rights have been a corner stone of economic growth. Now, with the rise of Open Source Design, or OSD, this paradigm is being challenged for the first time in human history.

Patent law is the law surrounding how one can secure a patent. A patent is simply a government backed document that guarantees exclusive rights to intellectual property. In the United States, patents have been the norm when it comes to securing intellectual property rights. The process for acquiring patents is lengthy, complex, and tedious. It can take several years to secure the exclusive rights to an idea using the patent system of securing your intellectual property. In the modern world, with technology evolving at break neck speeds, patents simply cannot keep up with the constant innovation. Furthermore, patents require huge legal departments to file and maintain them. This is an astronomical cost that cannot be footed by small businesses and start-ups, which are often at the center of the most innovative ideas.

Traditional companies benefit from the IP system as it allows them to block out competitors for selling similar products. So, for example, if a toy company came up with an innovative toy material that was more durable and child-friendly than any other material and they were able to secure a patent for that material, they could legally exclude all other manufacturers from using this material for a period of time. This would allow them to price and sell their toys with this new material with the safety net of knowing that no other company could match their product.

Technological companies don't benefit from this IP system. Given the ubiquity of technology, the collaborative nature of computer science, and the speed at which these innovations are made, it is impractical to try to patent every single idea as often times there is no way to fully exclude others from it as there are millions of implementations to any solution. Often times, creators and developers will even benefit from others using their solution as they will receive quicker feedback, analytics, and people may even improve on their design for them. Clearly, the tradition patent/IP system is failing this particular market.

Open Source Design provides a revolutionary alternative to traditional IP protocols. Open Source Design involves making your design and thoughts (your secret sauce) available to the world at large. So instead of hiding away your revolutionary idea until you secure a patent for it, you make it open to the public for everyone to use. The benefits of this concept are boundless.

One such benefit is the ability to have others contribute to your idea. Often times, with open sourced computer science projects, developers will 'fork' them. This is a colloquial term for taking an existing project, copying it over exactly, and them building your own version on top of it. So, if someone build a very innovative algorithm for machine learning and open sources it, then people can use that algorithm, change a few parts, and develop the capability to apply it in a totally new context.

Open source designs also allow for innovative ways to gather data and get information on consumers. This is innovative because consumers don't have to pay for the product or service they are using and companies don't need to pay exorbitant amounts of money to pay for data on consumers. Facebook uses data from every website that uses their login. This provides them valuable information on what other products their customers use so they can target advertisements even more specifically. This is only possible given that they open source their login capabilities. Open source design allows for a completely new level of information on each individual consumer and businesses are quickly realizing all of the potential for this data.

The inequality Crises: IP and OSD solutions

Inequality is the primary driver of the United States economy. The different abilities of individuals allow them to contribute value to one another's lives, thus creating value. Throughout much of the United States history, we were not a knowledge-based economy. Therefore, the fact that some information was restricted did not significantly contribute to inequality as much as rigid social structures. In the 21st century, the United States has shifted towards a knowledge-based economy. Now, information is what is valued and sought after by Western Society. Given this new paradigm, patents and the IP system are throttling the opportunity for others to improve their socio-economic status.

The benefits of the IP system with our knowledge-based economy is that it allows those with patents to achieve wealth like never before. However, these are clearly outweighed by the costs of limiting social mobility.

Open Source offers a compelling alternative to the outdated patent system. This new system would leave information unrestricted and allow the application of the information to be the primary driver of the world, still allowing for individuals to shine and rise. The democratization of information would clearly result in an inherently more balanced system which allows more growth to occur without penalizing those who worked for the knowledge. For example, Tesla does not file patents for much of their developments and they are still a thriving company. This case study displays the power of an open source solution.

An objection to Open Source would be that the system doesn't incentivize people to create new innovations and inventions because they don't have as much incentive to put in all of the effort required. While this objection has some merit, it falls short when faced with examples like Tesla and several other thriving open source companies and products.

Energy: IP and OSD solutions Compared

IP energy solutions have dominated the energy market for the last 150 years. Now open source energy exchanges and energy production techniques are edging their way into the market and may dominate the future of how energy is bought, sold, and produced.

One of the most powerful examples of open source energy done right is Grid+. Grid+ is a peer-to-peer energy solution built on the blockchain. The way it works is their device, when connected to your home grid, automates paying for electricity bills and the buying and selling of electricity over the grid in deregulated energy markets in the United States. Their software is used, most notably in the Brooklyn Microgrid, by independent home owners who produce their own power with renewables. Before, there was no easy way to sell energy back to the grid and no way to force energy companies to price more competitively. Now, with Grid+, residents in neighborhoods where Grid+ is active, users can not only sell energy back to the grid, but also buy energy from their peers, bypassing all other intermediaries. This is an incredible project and its software, and blockchain it is based on, is completely open sourced. [1][2]

Another powerful example of an open source energy solution is W.E. Power. W.E. Power is a blockchain based green energy platform. Their goal is to enable green energy producers to raise capital through offering energy tokens that have an inherit value that is energy. Their token and open source security models hold promise of decentralizing energy production and dropping prices for all parties involved while increasing revenues by cutting out distributors and middle-men. [3]

Tesla is the most built out open-source company in production. They have open sourced and released all of their patents. Their goal is to get their technology into as many hands in possible. This has several possible benefits. One benefit could potentially be to increase incentives for existing companies and governments to build out energy charging infrastructure. Another benefit would be using the research done by others based on the initial release of the patents would be available for Tesla to use. It also cuts down on the typical legal fees associated with filing for and protecting patents. [4]

One drawback of open source design solutions is that they don't allow for protections against larger companies and competitors. That is to say that without patents, it can be difficult for small businesses that stumble upon innovative ideas to gain a competitive advantage. This is a particularly powerful deterrent in the energy market-space which is naturally lent towards monopolies by the nature of grid-maintenance being outsourced to private companies in most countries. Challenging these major companies requires smaller start-ups to have their ideas protected so that they cannot be unfairly exploited and undercut by competition. Monopolies often stifle innovation as they have no need innovate beyond keeping the government regulators, that legally protect their monopoly.

Intellectual property rights also encourage investment. While there are certainly investment incentives under an open source model, most innovative technologies require patent protection for investors to reduce the risk of their investment and increase the capital injection. This increased capital injection allows for faster and more stable growth, increasing the likelihood of success. It is difficult to reproduce this investment security under an open source model.

An interest concept in green energy initiatives is windmill farms. Most companies buy their energy from the grid at standard pricing. If companies adopt the open source windmill technology, then they have the potential to save millions of dollars in energy costs. This is an example of incorporating open source hardware and software into an existing business model to create long term savings and reduced overhead in the years to come. [5]
