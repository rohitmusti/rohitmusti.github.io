Innovation and Product Development: IP and OSD solutions compared

Intellectual Property, or IP, has been the industry standard solution for creating new and innovative technologies. The definition of intellectual property is: property (such as an idea, invention, or process) that derives from the work of the mind or intellect. Intellectual property rights are important as they allow an individual to lay claim to their intellectual property which can then be used to profit off their ideas. For centuries, intellectual property rights have been a corner stone of economic growth. Now, with the rise of Open Source Design, or OSD, this paradigm is being challenged for the first time in human history.

Patent law is the law surrounding how one can secure a patent. A patent is simply a government backed document that guarantees exclusive rights to intellectual property. In the United States, patents have been the norm when it comes to securing intellectual property rights. The process for acquiring patents is lengthy, complex, and tedious. It can take several years to secure the exclusive rights to an idea using the patent system of securing your intellectual property. In the modern world, with technology evolving at break neck speeds, patents simply cannot keep up with the constant innovation. Furthermore, patents require huge legal departments to file and maintain them. This is an astronomical cost that cannot be footed by small businesses and start-ups, which are often at the center of the most innovative ideas.

Traditional companies benefit from the IP system as it allows them to block out competitors for selling similar products. So, for example, if a toy company came up with an innovative toy material that was more durable and child-friendly than any other material and they were able to secure a patent for that material, they could legally exclude all other manufacturers from using this material for a period of time. This would allow them to price and sell their toys with this new material with the safety net of knowing that no other company could match their product.
