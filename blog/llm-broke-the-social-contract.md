---
title: "LLMs Broke The Social Contract"
date: "2026-01-16"
---

LLMs have undoubtably changed the world.
In many ways this has been hugely positive (renowned mathematicians are using it to accelerate generating proofs [src](https://x.com/wtgowers/status/1984340182351634571)), but there have been negative repercussions too (a MIT study found that groups using LLMs fell behind in their ability to quote from essays they wrote just minutes earlier [src](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2506.08872v1)).


Something I haven't seen a lot of discussion about so far is how they are changing the social contract of writing and reading. 
Historically, if someone wrote something, they had put in significanatly more work into understanding material than the person reading.
Generally, this meant that if I read something and it was slightly confusing, it was likely because I hadn't put as much thought as the author into the specific are they are discussing.
LLMs have flipped this upside down.
Now that generating text is so cheap, it is now possible that the author who wrote something has put less work into understanding material than the reader.

An example of this is open source code contributions.
Some developers have thoughtfully used LLMs to engage and contribute to projects that would have otherwise been out of their reach (this is great!).
However, it is also common that folks have posted "AI slop" PRs or bugs.
These are contributions that the author has not put much thought into besides one or two prompts to their favorite LLMs.

One of the maintainers of CURL wrote up an analysis that 66% of reports to their bug bounty were neither a security issue or a regular bug [src](https://daniel.haxx.se/blog/2024/01/02/the-i-in-llm-stands-for-intelligence/).
They go on further to say that, historically, it was quite easy to filter out low quality/low effort security/bug reports. 
However, with the advent of LLMs, low quality/low effort security/bug reports often look extremely similar to high quality bug/error reports.
This means that folks were spending a lot more time double checking that issues/reports generated with AI and posted with realtively little effort are not legitimate.
This takes away from time and effort they could have spent making their systems more secure and stable.

LLMs are undoubtably useful and here to stay. 
However, we need new norms around writing and reading in a world where you can generate text/articles/blogs with less thought and care than you would spend reading it.
